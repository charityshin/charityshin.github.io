Spotify 2.0

Project Details


VIEW PROTOTYPE


Overview

Context

  • Type: Mobile Application Redesign
  • Duration: 4 weeks | 2017
  • Team: 4 members | research & design
  • Service: Spotify
Spotify has become a strongly playlist-driven service, as it consists of over 2 billion playlists, thanks to its many users who create playlist after playlist. Our team looked to Spotify as our redesigning focus. The process consisted of changing several aspects within the construction and managmenent of playlists. Spotify sifts through the metadata of playlists to compile even more playlists that are specifically geared towards users' preferences. These include songs that are genre-specific, mood-dependent, activity-reliant, or even environmentally contingent — all filtered under the unique umbrella of a user’s specific musical taste.

Problem

☁ How might we reconstruct a music-streaming application to create a more effcient playlist management system for users?

Although Spotify is a seemingly perfect platform, design is a continous journey; thus, certain design features within playlists management for specifically iOS users actually impede its them from fully enjoying the music experience. Through initial research, we found out that many users are hindered from curating Spotify playlists with efficiency due to its lack of clarity and discoverability in its current interactive features. We aimed to resolve these issues through careful research, analysis, and prototyping.

1st Phase

☉ Strategize: Problem & Goals

After deciding which application to redesign, we identified common interests and ideas in reconstructing Spotify’s weaknesses. For instance, the current playlist management function lacks the heuristic principle of user control and freedom, such as deleting songs from and adding songs to a specific playlist. So, we proceeded to work on formulating interview questions and planned to build scenarios from the interviewees.

Current Design


❥ Empathize: User Research

Creating a careful user interview guideline is essential to implement efficiency and good user experience in any design. Individually, we conducted interviews to three users. Initially, they were asked their preferred music applications. Below are some of the questions we asked our users; some questions and tasks were carried out spontaneously throughout the interviewing process.

    Interview Questions
  1. How long have you been using this music app?
  2. Do you use any other music apps other than this one? Which one and why ?
  3. Is your app updated to the latest version?
  4. When is the last time you made a playlist?
  5. What kind of playlists do you have?
  6. Why did you choose your preferred application? What do you like about this particular service. If they use other applications as well, what were some expectations and familiarities?
  7. Do you have any suggestions or improvements you recommend?
  8. What’s a feature that’s missing in your current music app that you wish existed in regards to making/managing playlists?
  9. Do you hear any new music app from friend/ or anywhere?

From this, we were able to discover intriguing findings, such as the fact that interviewees often do not pay attention to the extensive list of options, and rather, preferred to see clear choices in the form of large tiles (grid) that are seen in other parts of the application. A few even mentioned that they particularly liked the curated aspect of Spotify which made them use it more than other music applications that are often not as personalized (e.g. Soundcloud, Amazon Music). Based on those interviews, we created user scenarios based on 3 aspects: playlist creation, management, and other core activities within the mobile app.

    CREATING
  • ✦ Generic party playlist
  • ✦ Playlist for friend
  • ✦ Playlist of new releases
    MANAGING
  • ✦ Adding songs to existing playlist from EDIT
  • ✦ Adding relevant holiday songs from Holiday Playlist
  • ✦ Deleting songs frome existing playlist
    OTHER
  • ✦ Listening to playlist on shuffle
  • ✦ Reordering songs in playlist
  • ✦ Controlling where to queue a song


2nd Phase

✐ Analyze: Competitive Analysis

Since developing user scenarios, our redesign drew on aspects that we found well-designed from other music applications that use playlists. We constructed these ideas from comparing different workflows from those scenarios within different applications, (i.e. matching the playlist workflows from Spotify to those of Apple Music, Soundcloud, and Amazon Music). Thus, we focused mainly on the flow of creating a playlist based on existing playlists.

Example of Good User Workflows


We also analyzed competitors' navigation structures and genre classifications. With a clearer understanding of how users can discover and navigate through different music streaming services, we found out that Spotify lacks the ability to combine mixes that were curated. Our aim to improve on personalization directed most of our new features towards manipulating playlists that were curated or could be curated to each user. As of the tedious deleting process, we justified that swiping left and presenting a confirmation pop-up will be more sufficient and convenient for users.

❂ Design: Wireframing

The process of sketching and wireframing was essential as we went through many phases of redeveloping our ideas and learning from the obstacles we faced. Since our prototype focused on building based on users' preferences, we wanted to make sure to emphasize user control and freedom by implementing undo, cancel, and/or back buttons on every page throughout the process of curating a playlist. Our first sketch in the figure below overlooked these user control factors, but we soon realized that users should always be able to fix their mistakes or change their minds, especially when it comes to creating a playlist based on users' interests and needs.




3rd Phase

❀ Develop: Hi-Fi Prototyping

With our New Mix feature design, users can now create a playlist (known as a new mix) from songs chosen from the music application's algorithms that curate options such as: Your Recommendations, Your Playlists, and/or Genres & Moods. The key point of this feature is that users are able to easily and efficiently filter the songs inside a playlist to search for specific tracks that a user would want to add to their new mix. Once songs are selected, the application will generate a mix with these songs as well as songs that are similar to the selections - resulting in an automatically curated mix. This is because the generated mix is specifically based on songs that the user selects. The available filter preferences in the redesign include Title, Artist, Genre, Recently Added, and Most Played. As a result, users can easily access information on tracks such as the Artist and Genre without shifting through countless pages. Users are able to filter and sort by that information as a way to expedite their process to curate their desired playlist.

FINAL PROTOTYPE

❐ Evaluate: Design Decisions

Our newly thought-out interface addresses several heuristics that we sought could be improved in Spotify. We also tested the final prototype to our interviewees for feedback. After implementing the navigation bar change from "Radio" button to "Playlists" button, one of our test subjects said that "the process of viewing my playlist is a few interactions faster than before, which seems like not a big deal but is something I definitely appreciate."

Heuristic Evaluation
  • ✦ Flexibility and efficiency of use
  • ✦ Recognition over recall
  • ✦ Consistency and standards
  • ✦ User control and freedom
  • ✦ Error prevention

We also aimed to uphold the heuristic of consistency and standards which is important to maintain cohesiveness for clarity in the platform. This was due to the fact that interviewees often did not pay attention to the extensive list of options, as one individual stated, "The tiles are cohesive with some of the other parts of the application where people can view genres, which is really nice for aesthetics and clarity."

An example of a difficult layout decision was the placement of the "Delete" button when deleting multiple songs. We drew it out in both the top right corner (this touches on the familiarity of users with "Finish" buttons on the top right of most applications) and the bottom middle which may be more efficient as users will be scrolling down the playlist to delete their multiple songs. Based on the convenience factor, we decided to place the button on the top right as it is always accessible (as part of the header, it is a static bar that remains when the user scrolls downwards) whereas the bottom middle location would move based on where the user is located along the playlist.

Conclusion

❈ Aftermath

By executing a well-organized design process, we were able to enforce previously-mentioned decisions and solve major problems found through user research. This was done by implementing a 'Curate a New Mix' function and the option to delete song(s) more efficiently and conveniently. Because there is no such thing as a perfect prototype, we spent plentiful amount of time deciding and shifting to new improved concepts. By implementing these prototypes and ideas on Sketch and Invision, we were able to produce our final redesigned prototype.

☾ Final Thoughts

Overall, this project introduced me to the design process of mobile app development and expanded my passion for designing and implementing functional user experience aspects with eye-pleasing aesthetics. Ultimately, it's exciting to know there are limitless UX design adventures in regards to web/mobile usability and interface. Through this project, I learned to be more inspired by the bottomless opportunities in the never-ending world of design.
